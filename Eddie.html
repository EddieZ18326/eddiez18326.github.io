Soft Pneumatic Inspection Robot for Environmental Protection.

In the winter of 2019, a thunderous bang shook my family‚Äôs hotel room in Lake Tahoe. Lights flickered out, and a crowd rushed along the street. A gas pipeline had exploded. It caused only minor injuries, fortunately. But with more explosions in the news, including fatal ones near my grandparents‚Äô home, I started digging into the data. I was stunned: major incidents were occurring every 40 hours, releasing millions of tons of methane. The issue wasn‚Äôt a lack of awareness. It was, instead, weaknesses in existing technologies: slow and expensive, with systems incapable of detecting small leaks before they became catastrophic.

Current industrial methods for gas leak detection, including hydrostatic testing and camera inspection, fall short of meeting today‚Äôs needs. Pipeline leaks are both frequent and dangerous; in the U.S. alone, a significant leak occurs every 40 hours, with smaller fugitive leaks often going undetected and unreported, leading to millions of tons of methane emissions. Inspection approaches are often inaccurate, expensive, and labor-intensive, requiring extensive human involvement. Consequently, many leaks remain unnoticed for extended periods of time, all the while causing severe damage to both the environment and populations. These leaks endanger nearby populations, cause millions of dollars in losses, and contribute to the release of nearly seven million tons of methane into the atmosphere each year. Methane is uniquely concerning because it is 30 times more potent than carbon dioxide in terms of global warming potential, and it alone accounts for one-third of the world‚Äôs warming from greenhouse emissions. Therefore, early detection of gas infrastructure leaks has become an urgent and efficient way to reduce harmful emissions.

SPIRo (Soft Pipe Inspection Robot) is a pneumatically actuated soft pipe inspection robot for the accurate inspection of gas pipes in difficult-to-reach environments. It uses a compliant scissor linkage, McKibben artificial muscle actuators, and magnetic grippers. It achieves a speed of 15 mm/s when crawling on a horizontal surface and 9 mm/s when climbing up a vertical surface. It can also deform its body 60 degrees vertically and 40 degrees horizontally to clear obstacles. The robot‚Äôs origami-inspired grippers support a total weight of 417 grams of extra payload to account for the necessary leak detection sensors and equipment.

Also implemented on the robot is a multimodal deep feature fusion with a deep forest ensemble learning model to improve SPIRo‚Äôs gas leak detection accuracy and redundancy. The model consists of the convolutional layers of three CNNs, the AlexNet, ResNet-50, and MobileNet, and a deep forest ensemble model with all three CNNs. Not only is the ensemble model more accurate, but it is also more reliable due to increased redundancy in analyzing both thermal images and gas sensor data. In addition, the proposed method is also memory efficient, making it suitable for real-time detection applications.

I reached out to dozens of labs and professionals across the country to seek out advice for improving the robot to make it more suitable for field use. After several rounds of questioning and intervies, I was awarded an opportunity to conduct real-field testing at the Methane Emissions Technology Evaluation Center (METEC), thoroughly evaluating SPIRo‚Äôs real-world capabilities and achieving an accuracy of 95% throughout these tests. I am eternally grateful for METEC's generosity, who seeing as we were highschoolers, were able to waive the costs traditionally associated with testing on their site. I also worked with the MIT Media Lab to implement FlowIO, a standalone air compressor system on the robot and the Southwest Research Institute for calibrating the robot in tests at home.

I have co-founded a start-up, Enviro-AI, to bring this product to market.

Hydraulically Amplified Self-Healing Electrostatic Actuators for Personalized Medical Care

Cardiovascular diseases are the leading cause of death globally, claiming 17.9 million lives each year. Heart failure, one of the most serious variations of cardiovascular disease, is a chronic condition in which the heart is unable to pump enough blood to support the body‚Äôs needs. While simple lifestyle changes and medication can treat HF in its early stages, more serious cases, called class III or IV, often require more intensive treatment. For these scenarios, a heart transplant is the ideal treatment. However, the demand for donor hearts far exceeds their supply, leaving many individuals without access to it. Additionally, many individuals are deemed unfit for this process due to reasons such as age or other health conditions. These challenges underscore the urgent need for alternative solutions to address the growing issue of heart failure.

Soft robotics has emerged as a promising solution for the limitations of VADs. Unlike rigid robots, soft robots are composed of flexible, deformable materials that enable them to operate in complex, delicate, and unpredictable environments.I propose a cardiac assist system that integrates HASEL actuators with AI-based control to address the challenges of heart failure. The system demonstrates versatility, combining the flexible and safe characteristics of soft robotics with AI to advance VADs. The implementation of HASEL actuators with self-healing capabilities allows for increased reliability and performance. Using multi-material 3D printing ensures precise and reproducible geometries, which improves performance. The integration of machine learning for control allows the system to effectively address the nonlinear characteristics of HASEL actuators. Our algorithm achieves a R2 value of 0.91 in predicting stress. The device also achieves a maximum strain of 32.9% and a maximum energy output of 5.49 N‚Ä¢mm, exceeding requirements for cardiac assist applications. This work impacts medical care more broadly by making it more personal. with the use of DIW 3D printing and soft materials.

I am now founding a second start-up, Pulsar Robotics.

Deep-Water Shape Morphing Amphibious Robotic Turtle at Yale's Faboratory

Mobile robots are impactful for several different sectors, including search and rescue, environmental monitoring, and home service industry. Many mobile robots are designed to opperate in only one environment. As a result, their bodies are made up of dedicated mecahnisms for locomotion, such as paddles or wheels. Their behaviors are optimized only for that niche. Biological systems, however, exhibit phenoplasticity in their anatomical structure, stiffness, and overall behavior. This allows them to adapt and navigate multiple environments.

This shape-morphing amphibious robotic turtle is designed for locomtion across both terrestrial and aquatic environments. The limbs are inspired by those of sea turtles and land tortoises. They can transform between hydrodynamic flippers which are optimized for swimming and load-bearing legs that can allow for terrestrial mobility. This is achieved through pressure-responsive laminar jamming. This enables rapid and efficient shape and stiffness change.

Finally, to achieve deep-water swimming capabilities, the motors and chasis are designed to withstand up to 30 PSI of pressure, equivalent to a depth 10 meters. Furthermore, bouyancy control pumps are integrated to allow the turtle to achive neutral buoyancy or vary it's buoyancy to reach depths or ascend.

This project was conducted at Yale‚Äôs Faboratory, where I worked as a paid research assistant. I was invited to join the lab after presenting my work at RoboSoft 2025, which captured their interest.

Rockets I built as co-president of Aerospace club and RC Airplanes I designed.

After joining as a member of the Aerospace Club my freshman year, I was promoted to Co-President in 11th grade. I founded 2 rocketry teams to compete in the American Rocketry Challenge. I led the fundraising $2.5K from school administration through formal proposals and pitching. We designed a rocket, aiming to reach a height of 790 feet and return safely within the time limit, while carrying a payload of two raw eggs. We had a total of three launches, with two successful attempts. The remote control plane was custom built from foam board. It features gyro stabilization for smooth flight.

With little experience in rocketry, I recruited teammates to join my cause by providing a sense of direction, eventually forming two teams of 10. Over five months, I led the team through our first build process - doing reserach, proposing ideas, and eliciting feedback at each step. We competed our rocket days before our first launch, still not fully knowing precisely what to expect. On our first launch, our rocket miraculously flew straight as expected - a successful first launch. When we read over the altimeter data, we noticed our altitude had fallen slightly short of our goal, so we removed some weight. This, however, destabilized it for the second launch, causing it to careen across the field and shred itself to pieces. We left the launch site defeated but knowing we still had another launch day.

But that launch day would be next week, leaving us with 5 days to patch everything back together in time. I first called a meeting to assess what went wrong, and how we should bounce back. We quickly identified the problem: when we removed the weights, the center of gravity had shifted, throwing our rocket off balance. We pieced our rocket back together, using spare components and pieces we brought back. We brought it back to the field the next weekend, conducting two successful launches. On our third go, the rocket landed in a field of tall grass. When we were given the clear to recover it 10 minutes later, we couldn't locate it. That rocket probably won't turn up again, but its end, after everything we've been through, is oddly fitting. Our entire season has been about testing, rebuilding, and rising higher. We're now heading into our second year with far more experience and renewed determination.

FTC Robots designed from 2020 to 2023

I led the design and fabrication of three FIRST Tech Challenge robots from 2020 to 2024 (Freight Frenz, Power Play, Centerstage). All three are built around specific sets of rules, and they feature different approaches to manipulating game pieces to complete certain tasks, such as placing them on levels of shelves or stacking them on poles.

At the previous meet, our team had placed second out of 40 competitors, so confidence and morale were sky high. That is, until the chain for our robot‚Äôs virtual four-bar snapped mid-competition two weeks later, and its gripper‚Äîalong with my confidence‚Äîcracked in half upon impacting the floor. Later, upon analyzing the defeat, we discovered several flaws that had not been effectively communicated. I interpreted our robot‚Äôs failure as an extension of my leadership‚Äîand resolved to pick up the pieces, literally and figuratively.

I implemented a better communication system that allowed team members to report issues so that we could track which problems still needed addressing. This would improve collaboration between members, as those coming in to work on the robot after others had left would know exactly where to pick up as opposed to merely repeating the same work over and over, allowing us to track issues and solve them far more efficiently.
