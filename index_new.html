Soft Pneumatic Inspection Robot for Environmental Protection.

In the winter of 2019, a thunderous bang shook my familyâ€™s hotel room in Lake Tahoe. Lights flickered out, and a crowd rushed along the street. A gas pipeline had exploded. It caused only minor injuries, fortunately. But with more explosions in the news, including fatal ones near my grandparentsâ€™ home, I started digging into the data. I was stunned: major incidents were occurring every 40 hours, releasing millions of tons of methane. The issue wasnâ€™t a lack of awareness. It was, instead, weaknesses in existing technologies: slow and expensive, with systems incapable of detecting small leaks before they became catastrophic.

Current industrial methods for gas leak detection, including hydrostatic testing and camera inspection, fall short of meeting todayâ€™s needs. Pipeline leaks are both frequent and dangerous; in the U.S. alone, a significant leak occurs every 40 hours, with smaller fugitive leaks often going undetected and unreported, leading to millions of tons of methane emissions. Inspection approaches are often inaccurate, expensive, and labor-intensive, requiring extensive human involvement. Consequently, many leaks remain unnoticed for extended periods of time, all the while causing severe damage to both the environment and populations. These leaks endanger nearby populations, cause millions of dollars in losses, and contribute to the release of nearly seven million tons of methane into the atmosphere each year. Methane is uniquely concerning because it is 30 times more potent than carbon dioxide in terms of global warming potential, and it alone accounts for one-third of the worldâ€™s warming from greenhouse emissions. Therefore, early detection of gas infrastructure leaks has become an urgent and efficient way to reduce harmful emissions.

SPIRo (Soft Pipe Inspection Robot) is a pneumatically actuated soft pipe inspection robot for the accurate inspection of gas pipes in difficult-to-reach environments. It uses a compliant scissor linkage, McKibben artificial muscle actuators, and magnetic grippers. It achieves a speed of 15 mm/s when crawling on a horizontal surface and 9 mm/s when climbing up a vertical surface. It can also deform its body 60 degrees vertically and 40 degrees horizontally to clear obstacles. The robotâ€™s origami-inspired grippers support a total weight of 417 grams of extra payload to account for the necessary leak detection sensors and equipment.

Also implemented on the robot is a multimodal deep feature fusion with a deep forest ensemble learning model to improve SPIRoâ€™s gas leak detection accuracy and redundancy. The model consists of the convolutional layers of three CNNs, the AlexNet, ResNet-50, and MobileNet, and a deep forest ensemble model with all three CNNs. Not only is the ensemble model more accurate, but it is also more reliable due to increased redundancy in analyzing both thermal images and gas sensor data. In addition, the proposed method is also memory efficient, making it suitable for real-time detection applications.

I reached out to dozens of labs and professionals across the country to seek out advice for improving the robot to make it more suitable for field use. After several rounds of questioning and intervies, I was awarded an opportunity to conduct real-field testing at the Methane Emissions Technology Evaluation Center (METEC), thoroughly evaluating SPIRoâ€™s real-world capabilities and achieving an accuracy of 95% throughout these tests. I am eternally grateful for METEC's generosity, who seeing as we were highschoolers, were able to waive the costs traditionally associated with testing on their site. I also worked with the MIT Media Lab to implement FlowIO, a standalone air compressor system on the robot and the Southwest Research Institute for calibrating the robot in tests at home.

I have co-founded a start-up, Enviro-AI, to bring this product to market.

AI Copilot for Trauma Surgery

Access to life-saving humanitarian medicine in austere and resource-constrained environments is crucial to ensuring the health and safety of many around the world. However, access to high-quality medical care in these conditions is challenging due to the absence of connectivity and power, limited equipment, and the lack of available medical expertise. To address these constraints, we propose a memory-augmented Vision-Language Model (VLM) for action recognition and action anticipation to assist in medical decision-making and provide real-time intraoperative guidance to first responders and medical professionals performing life-saving interventions.

Algal Blooms research at SSTP

Harmful algal blooms (HABs), driven by environmental pollution, pose significant threats to water quality, public health, and aquatic ecosystems. This study enhances the prediction of HABs in Lake Erie, part of the Great Lakes system, by utilizing ensemble machine learning (ML) models coupled with explainable artificial intelligence (XAI) for interpretability. Using water quality data from 2013 to 2020, various physical, chemical, and biological parameters were analyzed to predict chlorophyll-a (Chl-a) concentrations, which are a commonly used indicator of phytoplankton biomass and a proxy for algal blooms. This study employed multiple ensemble ML models, including random forest (RF), deep forest (DF), gradient boosting (GB), and XGBoost, and compared their performance against individual models, such as support vector machine (SVM), decision tree (DT), and multi-layer perceptron (MLP). The findings revealed that the ensemble models, particularly XGBoost and deep forest (DF), achieved superior predictive accuracy, with R2 values of 0.8517 and 0.8544, respectively. The application of SHapley Additive exPlanations (SHAPs) provided insights into the relative importance of the input features, identifying the particulate organic nitrogen (PON), particulate organic carbon (POC), and total phosphorus (TP) as the critical factors influencing the Chl-a concentrations. This research demonstrates the effectiveness of ensemble ML models for achieving high predictive accuracy, while the integration of XAI enhances model interpretability. The results support the development of proactive water quality management strategies and highlight the potential of advanced ML techniques for environmental monitoring.

Rockets I built as co-president of Aerospace club and RC Airplanes I designed.

After joining as a member of the Aerospace Club my freshman year, I was promoted to Co-President in 11th grade. I founded 2 rocketry teams to compete in the American Rocketry Challenge. I led the fundraising $2.5K from school administration through formal proposals and pitching. We designed a rocket, aiming to reach a height of 790 feet and return safely within the time limit, while carrying a payload of two raw eggs. We had a total of three launches, with two successful attempts. The remote control plane was custom built from foam board. It features gyro stabilization for smooth flight.

With little experience in rocketry, I recruited teammates to join my cause by providing a sense of direction, eventually forming two teams of 10. Over five months, I led the team through our first build process - doing reserach, proposing ideas, and eliciting feedback at each step. We competed our rocket days before our first launch, still not fully knowing precisely what to expect. On our first launch, our rocket miraculously flew straight as expected - a successful first launch. When we read over the altimeter data, we noticed our altitude had fallen slightly short of our goal, so we removed some weight. This, however, destabilized it for the second launch, causing it to careen across the field and shred itself to pieces. We left the launch site defeated but knowing we still had another launch day.

But that launch day would be next week, leaving us with 5 days to patch everything back together in time. I first called a meeting to assess what went wrong, and how we should bounce back. We quickly identified the problem: when we removed the weights, the center of gravity had shifted, throwing our rocket off balance. We pieced our rocket back together, using spare components and pieces we brought back. We brought it back to the field the next weekend, conducting two successful launches. On our third go, the rocket landed in a field of tall grass. When we were given the clear to recover it 10 minutes later, we couldn't locate it. That rocket probably won't turn up again, but its end, after everything we've been through, is oddly fitting. Our entire season has been about testing, rebuilding, and rising higher. We're now heading into our second year with far more experience and renewed determination.

I interpreted our robotâ€™s failure as an extension of my leadershipâ€”and resolved to pick up the pieces, literally and figuratively.

Led hands-on STEM sessions at local Title 1 elementary schools using LEGO robotics and coding games.

When I was conducting robotics demos, I was approached by a teacher at Henry Ford Elementary who was interested in furthering the depth of robotics in her STEM program. She asked how to get students excited and shared her struggles. Listening, I realized that her problem was systemic: the curriculum was fixated on worksheets rather than wonder.

We began proposing changes. I introduced a hands-on Robotics curriculum and founded a district-wide FIRST LEGO League competition for eight teams. Rather than prescribing instructions, we encouraged testing unique ideas, and excitement overtook boredom as over 60 students huddled over sensors, cheered the robot on, and laughed at code going wrong.

We've now continued our program into the school year, having raised $4.5K to provide kits and materials for our classes and creating an after school enrichment program and LEGO robotics league for Henry Ford's students.

Led hands-on sessions building remote-control airplanes.

I led a remote-control airplane workshop at a local library, with 8 middle school students participating. I taught the class to build RC airplanes from scratch using foamboard. We then took a fieldtrip to a local flying park to test them together. But after hours of cutting and gluing, many of the class's planes came crashing down after wobbling just a few feet. I gathered the students around to try and identify problems, patching the battered airframes and wings while making adjustments based off of each test flight. Soon, my students began offering suggestions, changing the geometry of the wings on their own or shifting the center of gravity around, trying to identify the ideal setup. Our on-the-spot fixes weren't exactly pretty, but our planes flew beautifully through the air.
